Nobody else reads Gorham, Maine

The big data vendors cover New York and Chicago. Nobody is reading the 19,500 small and mid-sized municipalities that issue the same building permits, check registers, and assessor records, just on worse websites, behind more obscure FOIA statutes. The precursor documents that predict market events sit unread on those town hall websites. The system finds them because it has spent a year learning how to navigate these systems, one jurisdiction at a time. Every dead end teaches it something. The recipes accumulate.

How it works →

Known to locate certain things

A landowner in an unorganized territory in northern Maine sits on a cell tower lease worth $50,000 a year. The tower does not appear in the FCC's standard registration database. There is no municipal assessor, no town website, no CMS platform to scrape. The system found it anyway, by combining an FCC land mobile license, an FAA obstruction filing, a trail database entry for "Cell Tower Access Road," USGS elevation data, and a state tax roll. Five sources, none sufficient alone, each one a technique learned from a previous dead end somewhere else.

99% of these documents don't show up in search

Municipal CMS platforms store documents at URLs with no navigation link. A town clerk uploads a check register, it gets a URL, and nothing on the site ever points to it. Google can't index what it can't find. AI agents browse the same pages Google does. Roughly 99% of the documents ingested are invisible to search engines and AI assistants. They are public record, sitting on public servers, at URLs that nothing links to.

The system finds them because it has connectors for each municipal CMS platform, not a general-purpose crawler.
