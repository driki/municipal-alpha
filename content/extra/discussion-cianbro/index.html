The problem

A public project is decided long before it is a project. By the time it reaches an RFP, or a Dodge listing, the engineer is chosen, the design is set, and the remaining competition is on price. The decision that the job will happen, and the shape it takes, gets made months to years earlier: a line in a capital plan, a bond article on a town-meeting warrant, an EPA consent-decree program, a planning-board siting hearing. It is all public, in the narrowest sense of the word: a few people in a half-empty town room, and only if you knew the item was on the agenda at all. After that the record sits unread on one of thousands of separate municipal websites. So the builder finds out at the public event, on the same day as everyone else, or never.

What Municipal Alpha is

Every city, town, county, and district in the country publishes the decisions that move infrastructure, capital, and credit before they happen: the capital plan, the bond authorization, the permit, the siting hearing, the budget line, the vendor payment. Those records land months ahead of the public event, and they sit on thousands of separate municipal websites, in incompatible formats, where almost none of them reach a search engine. So the people those decisions affect find out late, on the same day as everyone else, or they never find out at all.

Municipal Alpha reads them the night they post, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies what matters, verifies every signal against the source document, and routes it to whoever needs it early. The job you would otherwise catch at the RFP, or never catch at all, arrives while there is still time to position for it.

The obvious question
"In a year, given the pace of AI, can't an agent just do this? Set an alert on a model and get the same thing?"

Fair question, and the honest answer starts by conceding the part that is true: reading a document and classifying it is the cheap step, and it probably does commoditize. The value is in the four layers underneath it that a model starting from a prompt does not have.

  • The corpus. A model with perfect extraction skills starting today still does not have 2010 through 2026 on Monday. The history has to be accumulating now to exist in eighteen months. It has been.
  • The hard part is before the model, and it is not AI work. Step zero is a registry mapping each local government to its actual digital footprint, the specific site or portal where its records live. Then you fingerprint that footprint to know which platform it runs (CivicPlus, Granicus, WordPress, Catalis, and a long tail) and how to pull from it. Then you extract, often from behind authenticated portals or PDFs indexed nowhere. Registry, fingerprinting, extraction: these are workflow and data-engineering problems, not LLM problems. The model only helps at the very end, once the document is already in hand.
  • A feed, not a question. You do not want to ask a model on Monday. You want something that fires the night the agenda posts. The product is a continuously running pipeline, not a query you have to remember to run.
  • The specific signal, and an honest one. Ask any model today which Maine towns are running a sewer-separation program under an EPA consent decree this year, or which New Hampshire towns are planning a bridge replacement for 2027. It cannot answer without the corpus and the resolved entities behind it, and if you ask anyway it will not tell you it does not know. It will confidently invent a town, a date, a citation. That is worse than no answer, because it looks like one. Every Municipal Alpha signal links back to the source document, so you get an answer you can defend instead of one you have to trust.

And if the bet is wrong and this does commoditize, the corpus accumulated by then is still the one a later entrant does not have. The cost of being early is overhead. The cost of being late is no corpus.

Show details for how the system reads it ▸

Every municipality in the country produces a stream of agenda packets, meeting minutes, capital plans, RFPs, bond warrants, siting hearings, and permit decisions. Most of them never get read by anyone outside the meeting room. The decisions that determine which infrastructure gets built, who is already positioned on it, and when the money comes together almost all start in those documents, months before they reach Dodge, a state DOT work plan, or the trade press.

Municipal Alpha reads those meetings the night the agenda drops, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies the documents, and follows the chain forward. A treatment-plant program shows up first as a line in a capital plan, then a consent-decree obligation, then an engineering contract, then a bid. A bridge shows up first as a culvert flagged at a CIP committee, then a design year, then a construction year. The system reads all of that, links it together, and surfaces the chain itself as the signal. For a builder, that is the layer ahead of Dodge and the RFP.
