Municipal Alpha for DESRI

A reference for the walkthrough. The short version: every town in New England is publishing the early evidence of where the next utility-scale project will live, which towns are turning friendly, which are about to slam the door, which competitors are quietly advancing, and almost none of it is searchable. Municipal Alpha reads it at scale, across thousands of municipalities, and turns it into a structured, dated, sourced feed. What follows is what the system reads, how it scores each signal into an action, the live New England read right now, and the questions where your view of DESRI Development's workflows would shape what a pilot looks like.

The thing your team can't see today

A utility-scale project leaves a paper trail for a year or more before it shows up anywhere an analyst would normally look. Planning-board agendas, EDA discussions, comp-plan amendments, overlay-district drafts, town-meeting warrant articles, draft moratoria, the earliest read on whether a town is being shopped, is friendly, or is about to freeze utility-scale solar. The catch: almost none of it is searchable, and the scale of where it hides is the point. The US has roughly 90,000 local governments (Census of Governments), and each one runs its own planning boards, zoning boards, and committees that publish separately, well over 100,000 distinct public bodies, none of it centrally indexed. The documents sit in tens of thousands of individual municipal sites, in PDFs and meeting minutes no crawler touches. You can't Google them, so you don't know they exist, and they're shaping where your next site can and can't go.

That is the gap the system fills. Not reading-the-document, DESRI already has people who can read a planning agenda. Surfacing the right document, at the right time, across thousands of towns at once, with the chain that leads to the utility load application pre-assembled. It's the same shape Palantir built a company on: you have a blind spot everyone in the field has, and the product is access to it.

What the system reads, layer by layer

Five layers, each a different upstream-or-downstream cut on the same project arc. The system reads them all and links across, so a signal isn't a loose document, it's a node on a chain indexed by town, project type, and competitor. This is the substrate that would feed your workflows.

  • Layer 1 · Municipal
    Planning board agendas + minutes, EDA + economic-development committee discussions, comp-plan amendments and overlay-district drafts, town-meeting warrant articles, sealed-bid RFPs, capital improvement plans, abutter-notice mailings. The earliest signal that a town is being shopped, friendly toward, hostile to, or wobbly about a utility-scale project. Six to twenty-four months upstream of the load application.
  • Layer 2 · State legislative
    Active bills shaping where utility-scale solar, BESS, and data centers can go, indexed by state and by topic. Solar and storage moratoria, interconnection-process reforms, data-center load classification proposals, siting-and-permitting legislation. The rules-of-play envelope before a project is sited.
  • Layer 3 · State PUC
    Renewable-energy siting and interconnection dockets, utility rate cases that contain capital improvement plans, infrastructure investment plans. Indexed by docket, municipality, and utility. Maine PUC, NH PUC, Massachusetts DPU, Connecticut PURA, Rhode Island PUC, Vermont PUC, NYSDPS. The layer immediately upstream of, and overlapping with, the utility load application.
  • Layer 4 · FAA OE/AAA
    Federal Obstruction Evaluation filings for structures above a height threshold. Solar disconnect and recloser poles, BESS substations, and interconnect towers surface here. Downstream of permitting, upstream of construction. The right-now read on which competitor projects have advanced past permitting and are about to build.
  • Layer 5 · Federal procurement
    SAM.gov solicitations indexed by agency and topic. Substation O&M contracts, federal data-center construction at DOE / VA / DoD facilities, BLM solar site readiness, transmission-corridor work. A cross-reference for federal-customer load growth that pulls private utility-scale capacity behind it.

From signal to action: the rung ladder

A raw document isn't usable in a workflow, your team would have to triage each one. So the system doesn't just surface the signal; it scores where the signal sits on the project chain relative to your decision window and assigns it a rung: an explicit recommended action plus the intel package that action needs. This is the piece I most want your read on, whether these four rungs map to how DESRI Development actually triages, and what you'd want in the package at each one.

  • ActInside the window, time-bound, do something now.
    A moratorium 90–180 days from a vote (reassign capacity, or get in the room to shape it). A load-application precursor (position generation ahead of the queue). A competitor about to build (know it before they're public). The signal carries a deadline and a named next step.
    who to contact · the deadline · what's at stake · the competitor / project / $ scale
  • TrackAdvancing and concrete, prepare, don't act yet.
    A planning-board site review scheduled, a draft ordinance circulating, a competitor's project entering site plan. On the watchlist; assign a developer, prep the engagement, or flag the competitor.
    project specifics · competitor identity · MW / $ scale · town-posture trend
  • MonitorEarly and upstream, low-confidence, watch.
    A town beginning to discuss solar/storage zoning, a comp-plan amendment floated, a bill introduced. No action yet; the signal earns a higher rung only if it advances.
    town-posture baseline · what would move this up a rung
  • SkipOutside DESRI's window or irrelevant, filtered out.
    Residential-only rezonings, sub-scale projects, jurisdictions you don't operate in. The rung that keeps the feed signal-dense instead of a firehose.
    , (the value here is what you don't have to look at)

One property of the rung worth naming: it's also an honesty check. You can't write a confident Act play for a thin or ambiguous signal, so the rung forces the system to be conservative rather than overclaim. A signal that can't earn a real action defaults down, not up.

A worked chain, applied to your shape of project

The canonical demo chain in the corpus today is a thirty-two-month tower-lease arc in Amherst, NH, traceable from one citizen comment to a unanimous five-zero ground-lease vote. The same shape generalizes directly to a utility-scale solar or BESS siting arc, and shows where each rung fires along the way.

  • Stage 1 · Citizen or developer-introduced item, Monitor
    A line in a Board of Selectmen minutes file: a resident raising the issue, or a developer's preliminary letter referenced in the agenda. Six to thirty months before the vote.
  • Stage 2 · Town-side institutional position, Monitor → Track
    Town counsel review, fire chief or DPW input, PILOT discussion, abutter notice. Where towns reveal their posture toward the project type before any formal application.
  • Stage 3 · Site plan review and Planning Board hearing, Track
    The first formal public read on the specific project at the specific site. Layer 1 surfaces both the agenda packet and the abutter responses.
  • Stage 4 · Town-meeting warrant article and vote, Track → Act
    In Maine, NH, and most of New England, the final authorization to lease or rezone goes through the warrant. The warrant text, the explanatory note, and the final tally are all extracted.
  • Stage 5 · PUC docket and load application, Act
    Layer 3 picks up where Layer 1 finishes. The PUC siting/interconnection docket lands, the load application is signed, the FAA filings for substations and interconnect poles follow. Layer 4 surfaces the construction-ready signal.

The chain reads forward across years, indexed by town, project type, and competitor, and the rung climbs as the chain advances toward your window. The question for the systems side is what the structured output looks like where it enters your workflows.

Where Municipal Alpha fits alongside what you already run

You manage DESRI Development's analytic and TI systems, so the honest framing is about fit, not replacement. Three things usually come up, worth naming them directly.

Against Blumen and RenUSA and similar tools. Blumen is the environmental-and-regulatory layer at the moment of permitting. RenUSA is the community-engagement layer once a site has been picked. Municipal Alpha is the municipal-government layer in between and earlier, the precursor read that precedes formal environmental review and community engagement by six to twenty-four months. If those are already in your stack, this is the upstream feed they don't cover, not a duplicate.

On integration. The output is structured and dated, signal, layer, town, source URL, rung, recommended action, so it's built to enter a workflow, not to be read as prose. However your team consumes intelligence today (a feed, an alerting layer, a warehouse table, a digest into an existing tool) is the right shape to discuss; the substrate is the same either way.

On "couldn't an LLM just do this." The LLM isn't the moat, and I won't pretend it is. The defensible piece is the inputs: the corpus, today spanning nearly 4,000 municipalities and built to scale into the rest of the ~90,000, the navigator infrastructure that scrapes every CMS variant in production, the classification pipeline tuned across a year of buyer feedback, the precursor-chain logic that links documents across years, and the FOAA back-channel for documents that aren't posted online. A general-purpose model doesn't have the corpus and can't assemble it cheaply, that's what you'd be integrating, and it's the part that's hard to rebuild in-house.
