Overview

What this is, in one breath

SMPDC serves 39 communities from Kittery to Stoneham, plus a role as the conduit for federal economic-development funding into the region. We read the same towns' public records the way a planner would, if a planner could read every agenda, minute, ordinance, and RFP in all 39 towns every week.

The corpus is national, roughly 3,400 municipalities across all 50 states, but the part that matters today is local: 25 of your 39 member towns are already in it, with Saco, York, Sanford, Biddeford, Wells, Kennebunk, South Berwick, Berwick, and North Berwick among the most active. Every item is sourced to the town's own published record, with a link, so you can trust it and re-derive it. Nothing here came from inside any town hall.

The other 14 are simply not stood up yet, and a quick check this morning says most of them run a standard town portal, so they are an onboarding backlog rather than a wall. Name the ones that matter to you and I can point the crawler at them. The genuinely hard case, the town with a part-time clerk who posts to a Facebook page or whose website lapsed years ago, is the more interesting one to talk about, because the way into a town like that is not a scraper. It is a note from a regional organization it already trusts. Which is to say, you. More on that below.

Problem

By the time it reaches your plate, it is usually fully cooked

This is the framing we have heard from economic-development leaders, not a diagnosis of SMPDC. Treat it as the menu of things we think we can help with, and tell us which ones are real for you and which ones miss.

A statewide pulse on selective groups, weekly or even monthly, to get ahead of anything before it ends up on our plate fully cooked. By the time the state relays something, we are not at the front end. You would learn things you would not learn otherwise, ever, not just late. An economic-development leader at a peer Maine region, in conversation with us

Three jobs kept surfacing, and they are different enough that they want different data:

Attraction
Matchmaking. Out of a long list of companies you might court, which ones are actually showing expansion signals, anywhere, so the outreach goes to the dozen that are real instead of the hundreds that are not.
Retention
Pre-distress warning. When a regional employer is heading for trouble, you usually learn it from the news, after it is decided. Retaining an existing employer is far cheaper than attracting a new one, but only if you see it early.
Credibility
Policy ammunition. Walking into a board fight, a funder, or a CEDS update with defensible comparables instead of assertions, whether the topic is a PILT split, school consolidation, or a TIF model.

Product

What Municipal Alpha actually is

Not a static dataset you buy once, and not a brief someone writes up by hand each cycle. A living read of municipal activity, kept current, delivered as structured data you assemble your own way.

01
Coverage map · which bodies we read in each town, council, planning, zoning, economic development, with doc counts and freshness. The honest denominator, including which towns are dark.
02
Precursor feed · new ordinances, rezonings, comprehensive-plan updates, RFPs, and decisions matching what you care about, surfaced as they post, each with a source quote and link.
03
Retroactive pull · the historical record for the fights and comparables, assembled on demand rather than reconstructed by hand.
04
Relevance feedback · you mark which items were useful, the feed sharpens, the noise drops, and what you flag stays private to you.
we find new activity→ surface it for review→ you mark what is relevant→ the feed learns your region→ noise drops

The important property for a regional planner: a town you name can be stood up quickly, because the engine is built to read any municipality that publishes. You define the keywords, the target sectors, the watch list. We do not need to know in advance what you will find valuable, which is the whole reason the relevance loop exists.

Where a pilot like this has started before

One Maine ED region is already shaping a version of this

A peer economic-development organization in the Katahdin region is defining a weekly read against their own target sectors and watch towns, on a nonprofit footing. The shape is the same one that would fit SMPDC: you supply the keywords, the target companies, and the towns; we supply the read and keep it current. It is deliberately light to start, because the value is in the feedback loop, not in a heavy build up front.

I am not trying to close a pilot on this call. I want to understand your region well enough to know whether the shape is even right, and what it would have to do to be worth your time.
