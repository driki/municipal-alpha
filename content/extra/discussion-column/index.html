The problem

"The introduction of legislation that may impact public notice statutes, which actions require notices to be published, and how."

Column, on the first thing it needs to track · June 8, 2026

A marketplace built on the legal requirement to publish a notice lives and dies by who changes that requirement, and when. And the change almost never starts as law. It starts as a bill in a statehouse, or as a quiet line on a planning-commission agenda proposing to amend the code: which paper qualifies, whether a website will do, what has to run at all. It is public, in the narrowest sense, a handful of people in a half-empty room, and only if you knew the item was on the agenda. After that it sits unread on one of thousands of separate government sites. So the business whose market it reshapes finds out when it is already law, on the same day as everyone else, or never.

90,000+ local governments, plus fifty state legislatures, decide who must publish a notice, where, and what it costs, in public, the night they meet. No team could read them all. Municipal Alpha reads more than three thousand of them, every night.

What Municipal Alpha is

Every city, town, county, and district in the country publishes the decisions that reshape public noticing before they happen: the code amendment, the procurement, the budget line, the move to a website. Those records land months ahead of the public event, and they sit on roughly twenty thousand separate government sites, in incompatible formats, where ninety-nine percent of them never reach a search engine. So the people those decisions affect find out late, or never.

Municipal Alpha reads all of them the night they post, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies what matters, verifies every signal against the source document, and routes it to whoever needs it early. The change to the notice rules you would otherwise catch when it is law, or the procurement you would catch at the RFP, arrives while there is still time to act on it.

What it reads
Agendas, minutes, ordinances, code amendments, RFPs and procurement items, budgets, and state legislation, the night they post, plus a predictive 50-state legislature monitor that catches bills three to six months before a municipality acts.
What it produces
Classified signals, each scored for confidence and linked back to the source record, assembled into the chain across time.
The method, not AI
A deterministic navigator and classification layer over a registry of where each government's records actually live. The model labels a document once it is in hand. It is not the moat.
The question a technical reader asks
"Given the pace of AI, can't an agent just do this? Point a model at the governments and get the same thing?"

Fair question, and the honest answer starts by conceding the part that is true: reading a document and classifying it is the cheap step, and it probably does commoditize. The value is in the layers underneath that a model starting from a prompt does not have.

  • The corpus. A model with perfect extraction skills starting today still does not have 2010 through 2026 on Monday. Tracking where the notice rules have already moved needs the history to exist. Ours has been accumulating.
  • The hard part is upstream of the model, and it is not AI work. Step zero is a registry mapping each government to its actual digital footprint, the specific portal where its records live. Then you fingerprint that footprint to know the platform it runs and how to pull from it. Then you extract, often from behind authenticated portals or PDFs indexed nowhere. Registry, fingerprinting, extraction: workflow and data-engineering problems, not LLM problems.
  • A feed, not a question. You do not want to ask a model on Monday. You want something that fires the night the agenda posts, across thousands of jurisdictions, continuously.
  • The specific signal, and an honest one. Ask any model today which counties amended their public-notice publication rules this quarter. It cannot answer without the corpus and the resolved entities behind it, and it will not tell you it does not know. It will invent a county, a date, a citation. Every Municipal Alpha signal links back to the source document, so you get an answer you can defend, not one you have to trust.

And if the bet is wrong and this does commoditize, the corpus accumulated by then is still the one a later entrant does not have. The cost of being early is overhead. The cost of being late is no corpus.

The chain: Maui County changes the rule for legal notices

One real chain from the corpus, the kind of change that moves your market, reconstructed to "what could we have known, and when." Every date is off the meeting record and every link was checked live before this page. The same method runs on any market you name.

  • Nov 12, 2025 · Molokaʻi Planning Commission
    The earliest signal. A staff report proposes amending the Maui County Code to change the legal-notice requirement from a newspaper "printed and issued at least twice weekly" to one "of general circulation," so the county can publish in either the Maui News or the Honolulu Star-Advertiser, "since the Maui News is currently less expensive." The draft ordinance is attached.

    The same ordinance goes to public hearing on the next island commission. No public testimony, the amendment keeps moving.
    View agenda →
  • Nov 25, 2025 · Maui Planning Commission
    The ordinance reaches the main island commission. Three commissions are now reviewing one change to the notice rules in the span of two weeks.
    View agenda →
  • Jan 28, 2026 · Molokaʻi Planning Commission
    The commission moves, seconds, and unanimously carries its recommendation on the ordinance. The change is now on its way to the County Council for the vote that makes it law.

The earliest knowable signal was the November 12 staff report, months before the change reaches the County Council and becomes enforceable. No press, no filing, the only record of this change to the notice rules lived in planning-commission packets, and the system read each one the night it posted.

Questions where your read changes what I build

Genuine discussion topics, not a checklist. Your answers define the product for this use case.

This is the load-bearing question. It decides whether the deliverable is a strategic watch on the notice rules, a lead feed for your sales team, or both, and it is the difference between a useful tool and a data dump.
Coverage: which markets matter most, your newspaper partners' footprints, specific states, or national, and which record types do you want first?
Your partners run multi-state. Naming the markets and the record types keeps a pilot scoped to what moves your business, instead of a national firehose.
Depth: do you want the change itself, the bill or the RFP, or the whole chain from the first committee mention to adoption?
The Maui chain is four meetings deep. If you only need the final ordinance, that is a lighter product, if you need the early warning, the depth is the point.
Recency: a one-time map of where the rules are changing, or an ongoing feed the night it posts? What does a week late cost you on a procurement lead?
The three vectors price the work. A one-time backtrack and a daily feed are different products, and the cost of being a week late on a lead tells us which cadence is worth paying for.
The two we would shape most: how would you want the "this town is struggling with manual publication" signal to look, and is notice volume something you already hold from your own marketplace?
These are the fuzzier two of your four. Defining them with you, rather than guessing, is what keeps the product honest about where it is sharp and where it is a search we are tuning.
