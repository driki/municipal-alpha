The problem

"The introduction of legislation that may impact public notice statutes, which actions require notices to be published, and how."

Column, on the first thing it needs to track · June 8, 2026

A marketplace built on the legal requirement to publish a notice lives or dies by who changes that requirement, and when. In Maine that change already has a number. LD 2042, "An Act to Eliminate the Requirement for Municipalities to Provide Public Notice in a Newspaper," is moving through the legislature now. The bill itself is one record. The towns that will have to rewrite their own ordinances when it passes are hundreds of others, each posting the news on its own site, on the night it meets. A company whose market the bill reshapes usually finds out when it is already law, on the same day as everyone else, or never.

90,000+ local governments, plus fifty state legislatures, decide who must publish a notice, where, and what it costs, in public, the night they meet. No team could read them all. Municipal Alpha reads more than three thousand of them, every night.

What Municipal Alpha is

Every city, town, county, and district in the country publishes the decisions that reshape public noticing before they happen: the code amendment, the procurement, the budget line, the move to a website. Those records land months ahead of the public event, and they sit on roughly twenty thousand separate government sites, in incompatible formats, where ninety-nine percent of them never reach a search engine. So the people those decisions affect find out late, or never.

Municipal Alpha reads all of them the night they post, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies what matters, verifies every signal against the source document, and routes it to whoever needs it early. The change to the notice rules you would otherwise catch when it is law, or the procurement you would catch at the RFP, arrives while there is still time to act on it.

What it reads
Agendas, minutes, ordinances, code amendments, RFPs and procurement items, budgets, and state legislation, the night they post, plus a predictive 50-state legislature monitor that catches bills three to six months before a municipality acts.
What it produces
Classified signals, each scored for confidence and linked back to the source record, assembled into the chain across time.
The method, not AI
A deterministic navigator and classification layer over a registry of where each government's records actually live. The model labels a document once it is in hand. It is not the moat.
The question a technical reader asks
"Given the pace of AI, can't an agent just do this? Point a model at the governments and get the same thing?"

Fair question, and the honest answer starts by conceding the part that is true: reading a document and classifying it is the cheap step, and it probably does commoditize. The value is in the layers underneath that a model starting from a prompt does not have.

  • The corpus. A model with perfect extraction skills starting today still does not have 2010 through 2026 on Monday. Tracking where the notice rules have already moved needs the history to exist. Ours has been accumulating.
  • The hard part is upstream of the model, and it is not AI work. Step zero is a registry mapping each government to its actual digital footprint, the specific portal where its records live. Then you fingerprint that footprint to know the platform it runs and how to pull from it. Then you extract, often from behind authenticated portals or PDFs indexed nowhere. Registry, fingerprinting, extraction: workflow and data-engineering problems, not LLM problems.
  • A feed, not a question. You do not want to ask a model on Monday. You want something that fires the night the agenda posts, across thousands of jurisdictions, continuously.
  • The specific signal, and an honest one. Ask any model today which counties amended their public-notice publication rules this quarter. It cannot answer without the corpus and the resolved entities behind it, and it will not tell you it does not know. It will invent a county, a date, a citation. Every Municipal Alpha signal links back to the source document, so you get an answer you can defend, not one you have to trust.
  • It gets sharper with use, and that compounds across customers. This is the part a model answering a cold prompt never reaches. Each customer tells the system which signals actually mattered to them, the way a streaming service's weekly picks sharpen the more people listen, and that calibration carries forward into what the next reader sees. The more Column tunes it toward public-notice signals, the better it gets at public-notice signals, for Column and for whoever comes after, and a later entrant cannot buy the years of calibration that built it.

And if the bet is wrong and this does commoditize, the corpus accumulated by then is still the one a later entrant does not have. The cost of being early is overhead. The cost of being late is no corpus.

Two layers, and why you want both

Everything you raised does two things. One finds you new accounts to sell into. The other is a standing watch, so a change in the rules is not something that catches you off guard.

The opportunity feed
Where to sell, town by town
The procurements, the annual official-newspaper designations, the towns openly moving off paper, scoped to your markets and surfaced as your team works a region. This is all the small towns you could never afford to chase one at a time, in one place. The list for any region is finite, and that is the point: you can work through it, prove it is worth it, and know who to call first.
The standing watch
The next LD 2042, before it is law
The 50-state legislative and local-code monitoring of public-notice requirements, continuous. A bill that drops the newspaper mandate is an existential hit to one side of your marketplace, and this layer catches it forming, in every statehouse, not on the day it passes. A lead feed for a region eventually runs out, the monitoring does not.

Maine is not the only statehouse

The newspaper-to-website shift is a national wave, and the standing watch is already catching it in other states, bill by bill, the same way it caught LD 2042.

Same shape, other statehouses
The wave, caught at filing
Oklahoma HB4173 would move school-district notices from newspapers to the State Department of Education's website. Wisconsin SB162, enacted as 2025 Act 70, adds a bid requirement for printing county-board notices and advertisements. Each was flagged by the legislature monitor as it moved, not after it passed.

Across the 174 Maine municipalities I cover, 23 carry public-notice publication-rule language and roughly 40 itemize what noticing costs, from Hermon's budgeted advertising contract to towns now reacting to LD 2042. That is every town you could never afford to prospect one by one, in one place.
