League City, TX (Galveston County): a fiber-saturated suburb where the competitive front has already moved

The read. League City is a built-out Houston-metro suburb where the broadband market is already three-deep and the fiber race is live. The cable incumbent, Xfinity, reaches essentially the whole city; the differentiator is fiber, and Frontier has pushed symmetrical fiber across the large majority of the city while AT&T fiber covers most of it. For your team, the read is that this is not a "win the first build" market, it is a "win the next franchise/ROW cycle and the anchor-institution accounts" market: the council's infrastructure and right-of-way governance is where the next move happens, and the local body that touches it is meeting now. None of this comes from BEAD subsidy: a city this served is funded by private competition, so the public-record angle here is ROW, franchise terms, and the council's posture toward the providers already in the ground.

The deliverable is not a report I write up each cycle, it is a weekly watch on the Texas jurisdictions you care about: the permitting and infrastructure activity that maps to broadband, surfaced while it is still an agenda item rather than a denial letter. New items get introduced as I find them, the existing ones get a weekly refresh with a status, and that weekly change is what you consume. It arrives two ways.

Mode one: the email digest

A readable weekly digest your field and program teams can scan: each item with where it sits, who decides, and why it matters to broadband, scored for relevance and carrying a status as it moves toward a clock or an award.

Mode two: the CSV that joins your systems

The same items as a structured weekly pull, a CSV or read-access feed, that drops into the Texas pipeline and tooling you already run rather than asking your team to rebuild anything. (you mark it). The status and last-updated on every item are the early warning: you see a permit drifting toward the 90-day clock, or a project advancing from planning to procurement, while there is still a decision to influence.

How you'd steer it

The feed defaults to relevant. Every item ships marked useful by default, so there is nothing you have to do for it to work. When something misses, you mark the Useful column and drop the file back in the same shared location, and that one signal sharpens the next week to your pipeline. It works like training a Netflix recommendation feed, except it is tuned on what Ready.net actually pursues. Low-friction by design: the loop runs on your silence, and only sharpens when you choose to correct it. What you flag as relevant stays yours; only the generic real-signal-versus-noise quality signal is ever shared.

3How we'd measure it, and whose job is what

I should be graded on the intelligence I deliver, not on your internal results, which ride on your own pipeline, capital, and relationships. So success is measured on three things, each one only on what I can actually control, so the end evaluation is arithmetic instead of a gut feel.

What I am on the hook for

  • Coverage of the agreed scope, your named Texas jurisdictions plus the surrounding ring, with no silent gaps, every claim sourced to the town's own primary record rather than a tracker or a summary. Measured as the share of your target jurisdictions actively monitored with at least one relevant signal in a trailing 12-month look-back. The look-back matters because the pilot runs over a Texas summer recess, so anchoring on in-window yield would penalize us for a town's calendar, not our reach. Target is yours to propose once we both see the jurisdiction list.
  • Relevance, the feed tunes toward what you flag as useful; the feed's quality is mine even though the flags are yours. Read always next to completeness, the small-jurisdiction items you would not otherwise have seen, so the feed cannot inflate its useful-rate by narrowing to safe, obvious items.
  • Earliness, catching real changes with enough lead time to act. The bar I propose, because I know where the public record lands, is at least one change caught at agenda stage or earlier, which is weeks of runway before the binding vote or the award, not a same-day ping and not a quarter's notice. This pilot should clear it on my own ground.

The rolling scorecard. These three, coverage, relevance, and earliness, are not a one-time end-of-pilot reckoning. Each weekly digest carries them at the top as a small rolling scorecard, so terms-met is continuously visible rather than argued at day 90. By the time we get to the conversion decision the numbers are pre-validated, you have watched them every week, and the auto-roll below is just confirming what the scorecard already showed.

How earliness is measured, and validated. Lead time is arithmetic from dated documents, not a judgment: the gap between when I flag a change at its earliest public artifact (a hearing scheduled on an agenda, a grant application, a 7460-1 inside its pre-build window) and the binding decision (the vote, the RFP, or the award). Both dates come from the documents, so the number is auditable, not asserted. I validate it two ways, both on my own ground. First, a retrospective backtest: name two or three known past Texas outcomes and I reverse-engineer the precursor chain from public records, dates straight from the record, provable today without you lifting a finger. Because you pick the case, it cannot be cherry-picked. Second, a forward catch during the pilot: at least one real change surfaced at its early stage with a documented lead time, logged before the outcome and scored after, so there is no crediting hindsight. The backtest proves method and lead; the forward catch is the only test that cannot cheat. Neither needs you to confirm it helped.

Whose job is what

I am accountable for coverage, sourcing, and lead-time. You co-own the relevance feedback, a few minutes when something misses. I am not gated on your hit-rate, your deal count, or your outcomes, those ride on your own pipeline, capital, and relationships, and I would be suspicious of any vendor who claimed them. I will track and report the contribution, but I will not ask to be gated on an outcome I do not own.

The default-true consequence. Because every item ships marked useful, your silence means the terms are being met. There is nothing you have to send back for the watch to count as working. The only thing that triggers a conversation is the reverse: if a meaningful share comes back marked not-useful, that is the check-in, we sit down and ask why that share is missing, and re-point the read. Silence is success; a run of misses is the signal to recalibrate.

If I do not deliver the coverage and the lead-time catches, we do not roll forward. That is the line, and it is one I can be held to without reaching into your results.
