Before the mechanics, the structure: the four questions split into three layers by who controls what, so we both know what each of us is on the hook for.

My end (measurable, and it is mine)

This is what you hold me to: coverage of your named jurisdiction list, no silent gaps; relevance tuning, you flag what's useful and the feed tunes toward it, the feed's quality is mine even though the flags are yours; the discovery lead on real public events, date math that can't be inflated; completeness, the items from small jurisdictions you wouldn't otherwise have seen; and that every item carries a why-it-matters, if I can't name what you'd do with it, it doesn't belong in the feed. One thing I'm deliberately not doing: chasing a "useful score" by sending only safe, obvious items. That would quietly cost you the small-jurisdiction catches that are the whole point, relevance and completeness move together. What's shared is whether you act on it, and that's the next tier.

The shared proof (the one we read together)

You're acting on it: the acted-on rate (your Relevance question) and the net-new opportunities you'd actually pursue. Both take your eyes, your flags and the blind reconciliation against your pipeline, so I'm not gated on them alone, but they're the truest signal the thing is working.

Your outcomes (yours to close, I report the contribution)

Pipeline opened, deals won, a state customer renewed where the feed played a part. I can't close those for you, that's your team and your relationships, but I'll show where something we surfaced was the early thread, so the chain from signal to outcome stays visible. Those numbers never sit on my scorecard, and I'd be suspicious of any vendor who claimed them.

The mechanics of each question, below.

1. Coverage (is there usable signal volume across the jurisdictions that matter?)

  • Metric: jurisdiction coverage ratio = (named target jurisdictions actively monitored AND with ≥1 relevant signal in a trailing 12-month look-back) / (your named target list, frozen at delivery). Measures reach to proven signal-producing jurisdictions, separate from whether a town happens to meet during a summer window.
  • Target: yours to propose at kickoff, once we both see the jurisdiction list. A fair bar depends on how many of your targets publish a rich online record versus a thin rural one, so neither of us should pick the number blind.
  • Why look-back: the pilot runs July to August, peak summer recess for small munis. Anchoring on in-window yield would penalize us for a town's calendar, not our reach. In-window yield is reported separately; the live, in-window proof is carried by Earliness + Net-new.
  • Carve-outs (mutual, not scored against us): a named entity with no crawlable public record; a jurisdiction that posted nothing during the window.
  • What we need from you: (a) the TX jurisdiction list that matters, the Watch jurisdiction rows of the calibration sheet, which becomes the frozen denominator; (b) your current TX coverage today, so we know whether Coverage is even where the value lies. The list needs to be enough to start, not exhaustive, put in the jurisdictions that matter most, and we treat it as the baseline we tune from, not the finished article.

2. Relevance (what share of signals are genuinely useful?)

  • Metric (behavioral): acted-on rate = share of delivered signals you engage with or advance (opened or exported via the read-access feed, entered into pipeline, or pursued), reconciled against the pipeline at mid-pilot and end. A signal you use counts as relevant regardless of any label.
  • Tuning input (not a trigger): a useful? y/n/note field stays, but only to tune next week's feed, separate from any gate, so there's no incentive to under-mark a signal.
  • Anti-loophole: any signal that appears in your pursued pipeline counts as useful even if marked not useful. Use overrides the checkbox.
  • The counterweight: relevance is always read next to completeness, how many items came from the small jurisdictions and filings you wouldn't otherwise have seen. A feed can inflate its useful-rate by narrowing to safe, obvious items; pairing the two means gaming one tanks the other, and you'd feel the thinning feed immediately.
  • Target: calibrated at kickoff once delivery is set (read-access or API makes engagement observable; an opaque CSV does not).
  • What we need from you: 2-3 examples of what genuinely useful looks like, and read-access or API delivery so engagement is observable.

3. Earliness (how much lead time do the signals give?)

Earliness is two claims, and they need two proofs. Splitting them is the honest way to measure it, and it's where most backtests quietly cheat.

3a. Discovery lead, provable day one, no hindsight

  • Metric: realized lead (days) = (objective public downstream event: RFP, permit, or award date) minus (the timestamp our system logged the precursor, detected_at, written the moment we classify it). Two dated facts, neither reconstructed. Plus a count of cases ≥ 60-day and ≥ 90-day lead.
  • Target: yours to propose at kickoff. For scale, the FAA-filing class alone carries a built-in 45-day-plus pre-construction lead, and planning-stage precursors lead by months, so the data gives us a visible floor to set it against.
  • What it proves, and doesn't: that the precursor was in the record, and in my feed, well before the public event, and how large the lead is. It does not by itself prove a hit rate, because the cases are picked already knowing the outcome. That's what 3b is for.

3b. Predictive hit rate, proved forward, inside the pilot

  • Metric: of the precursors I flag forward-looking during the pilot (this points at an RFP, permit, or award, ~N days out), the share that resolve into the named event, each scored when it settles. Logged before the outcome, scored after, so there's no crediting hindsight.
  • Target: calibrated at kickoff, reported as a running tally, since few precursors settle inside a short summer window (one reason I'd recommend 90 days, see Section 4).
  • Why it's the rigorous one: it's tempting to label a document this-meant-X-was-coming when you already know X happened. The only test that can't cheat is one where the prediction is on the record before the outcome is. That test is the pilot.
  • What we need from you (the easy ask): name 2-3 known past TX outcomes (a project, RFP, or award that happened). I reverse-engineer the precursor chain from public records for the 3a proof; because you pick the case, it can't be cherry-picked. We do not ask you to reconstruct internal we-learned-on-X dates.
  • On the window: precursors lead by months and the pilot runs over a slow summer, so if earliness is the metric that matters most, the honest fix is to size the window to settled outcomes (90 days, or 60 signal-active days), not to lean harder on look-backs. The 3a discovery-lead proof is there day one regardless.

4. Net-new (does it surface opportunities you weren't tracking?)

  • Metric: net-new count = items you flag as both new to you and worth pursuing, by blind, client-side reconciliation: you mark each delivered item on your side and report the tally, your pipeline never leaves your hands. (The "worth pursuing" is what separates this from Relevance: new and actionable, not just unseen.)
  • Target: calibrated at kickoff, anchored to what an opportunity is worth to you, not a generic count. For a book where individual deals are large, even one net-new opportunity you'd otherwise have missed can clear the cost of the pilot, so we set the number together rather than assert one. Reported as a running tally.
  • What we need from you (low-exposure): roughly what one "opportunity" looks like for Ready.net and what it's worth, so the target means something, plus the new-or-known flag on the items I deliver. Zero-exposure fallback: net-new measured against the public channel you rely on.

Mutual participation. A pilot is a two-party experiment, and the feedback loop is the part that makes it work. I deliver and tune; you spend a few minutes a week on what landed. That cuts both ways: you get better results when you tell us what landed, and I get a better product from it, so it's mutual, not a favor. We name the light inputs (the kickoff inputs, the mid-pilot check-in, the weekly feedback), kept light and non-punitive: if a week slips, the clock pauses and the subjective metrics fall back to passive telemetry, never a penalty. The objective metrics still give a clean shared read.
