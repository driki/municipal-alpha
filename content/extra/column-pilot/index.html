The pattern worth knowing. A town adding website or Facebook notice today is the leading indicator of dropping print tomorrow. The drift starts as an "and," the newspaper and the website, and becomes an "instead of." That is the moment that costs a public-notice business, and it shows up in the minutes before it is policy. A state bill tells you a requirement might change somewhere; it does not tell you which of your towns is doing it, or what they pay, or who to call.

  • New York, Lackawanna: the official newspaper, and the channels stacking around it

    Lackawanna · Erie County, NY
  • Missouri, Cameron: the newspaper, the website, and Facebook
    The council record reads, "Notices were published in the Cameron Citizen Observer, the City's website and the City's Facebook page."     Cameron · Clinton County, MO
  • Arizona, the notice-placement rule that decides where your ad runs
    An Arizona municipal notice document spells out the format rule that governs legal advertising: a Truth in Taxation notice "must be published twice in a newspaper of general circulation in the city/town, in a location other than the classified or legal advertising section." The kind of placement-and-format requirement that decides where, and whether, a notice runs in print. Source: League of Arizona Cities and Towns notice guidance.
    Arizona · statewide notice rule
  • Kansas, Fairway: the public notice, and the paper it ran in
    A zoning-board packet records that "The Public Hearing was published in the January 6, 2026, edition of The Legal Record" with notices also mailed to nearby owners. The legal-notice paper is named and dated, straight from the town's own packet. Source: Fairway zoning board packet.
    Fairway · Johnson County, KS

Why the snapshot is the photo, and the watch is the movie

Everything above is a snapshot, a moment caught in the record. The value is not the snapshot, it is the movie: the same towns watched over time, so the change is caught as it happens.
