How to ask it
Map the spread of mentions of [topic] across [region or state], with dates and document types. Where did it appear first? Which towns are picking it up now? What language are they using?

This question is asked by macro funds, policy researchers, infrastructure REITs anticipating regulatory friction, and operators evaluating which markets to enter or avoid. The early movers in municipal records are the leading indicator for state-level action, the same way Massachusetts towns banned indoor smoking eight years before the state did.

The corpus tracks topical mentions across every municipality in the pipeline with date, document type, and the surrounding context. The buyer gets a contagion map: where it started, where it's spreading, what the friction looks like at each stage. The Maine data-center example below traces the public-record signal back through municipal planning boards and citizen complaints two years before the state-level moratorium discussion.

Answer view, data from the corpus

Maine data-center moratorium, the signal in town halls before the state vote. On April 6, 2026, Maine's House passed LD 307, which would have become the country's first state-level moratorium on large-scale data centers, on an 82 to 62 vote. The Governor vetoed it on April 24; the override attempt failed April 29. The moratorium itself died, but on the same day Maine signed LD 713 (banning data-center tax incentives) and an executive order establishing the Maine Data Center Advisory Council.

The state-level fight was visible in town halls five months earlier: six Maine town halls (out of 173 Maine municipalities the corpus monitors) began discussing data-center restrictions independently, without coordinating. The corpus surfaced the early-stage discussion at the local level before any state-level body had drafted a moratorium proposal.

What this shows. Five months of public-record signal across six independent Maine towns, surfacing the same friction, before any state-level legislation. The same shape plays out for any topic that hits municipalities before it hits states. Data centers are the worked example because LD 307 is recent and well-documented. The pattern repeats across short-term-rental zoning, PFAS treatment requirements, AI workplace policies, and any other topic in the contagion class. For some topics the lead time runs months, for others (Massachusetts indoor-smoking bans from 2002 to 2010 statewide) it runs years. The corpus is the listening device; the lead time is the variable.
