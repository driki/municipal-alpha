The problem

A public water or wastewater project is decided long before it is a project. By the time it reaches an RFP, the program is scoped, the budget is set, and very often an engineer has already been advising the town on it for a year. The decision that the work will happen, and the shape it takes, gets made months to years earlier: a line in a capital plan, a State Revolving Fund application, an EPA or state consent-decree obligation, a PFAS pilot at a water district, a sewer-separation program on a finance-committee agenda. It is all public, in the narrowest sense of the word: a few people in a half-empty town room, and only if you knew the item was on the agenda at all. After that the record sits unread on one of thousands of separate municipal websites. So the firm that was not already in the room finds out at the RFP, on the same day as everyone else, or never.

What Municipal Alpha is

Every city, town, county, and district in the country publishes the decisions that move infrastructure, capital, and credit before they happen: the capital plan, the State Revolving Fund application, the bond authorization, the permit, the siting hearing, the budget line, the vendor payment. Those records land months ahead of the public event, and they sit on thousands of separate municipal websites, in incompatible formats, where almost none of them reach a search engine. So the people those decisions affect find out late, on the same day as everyone else, or they never find out at all.

Municipal Alpha reads them the night they post, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies what matters, verifies every signal against the source document, and routes it to whoever needs it early. The obvious question
"In a year, given the pace of AI, can't an agent just do this? Set an alert on a model and get the same thing?"

Fair question, and the honest answer starts by conceding the part that is true: reading a document and classifying it is the cheap step, and it probably does commoditize. The value is in the four layers underneath it that a model starting from a prompt does not have.

  • The corpus. A model with perfect extraction skills starting today still does not have 2010 through 2026 on Monday. The history has to be accumulating now to exist in eighteen months. It has been.
  • The hard part is before the model, and it is not AI work. Step zero is a registry mapping each local government to its actual digital footprint, the specific site or portal where its records live. Then you fingerprint that footprint to know which platform it runs (CivicPlus, Granicus, WordPress, Catalis, and a long tail) and how to pull from it. Then you extract, often from behind authenticated portals or PDFs indexed nowhere. Registry, fingerprinting, extraction: these are workflow and data-engineering problems, not LLM problems. The model only helps at the very end, once the document is already in hand.
  • A feed, not a question. You do not want to ask a model on Monday. You want something that fires the night the agenda posts. The product is a continuously running pipeline, not a query you have to remember to run.
  • The specific signal, and an honest one. Ask any model today which Maine towns are standing up a PFAS treatment facility this year, or which New Hampshire towns have a sewer pump station scheduled for 2027 construction. It cannot answer without the corpus and the resolved entities behind it, and if you ask anyway it will not tell you it does not know. It will confidently invent a town, a date, a citation. That is worse than no answer, because it looks like one. Every Municipal Alpha signal links back to the source document, so you get an answer you can defend instead of one you have to trust.

And if the bet is wrong and this does commoditize, the corpus accumulated by then is still the one a later entrant does not have. The cost of being early is overhead. The cost of being late is no corpus.

What your firm already does, and what this doesn't

The honest version, because the limits are the credibility.

Build vs. buy
"We already watch our market. Our people hear about this work."

They do, and this does not replace them. Your business developers know your key towns, your past clients, and the people who call when something is moving. That coverage is real, and it is narrow by design, it follows the relationships you already have.

What your people can't do is read every agenda for the hundreds of towns you aren't watching. They can't catch the line item in a finance committee packet that flags a wastewater capital plan, months before it becomes an RFP. That's the gap, finding the work in towns where you don't have a relationship yet, without hiring more people to do the reading.

What it won't do
"So this wins us the work?"

No, it won't win you the work, and any tool that says it can is overselling. Municipal Alpha surfaces the signal and the sourced record early. It does not write the proposal, carry the relationship, or set the price. Whether an early signal becomes a win rides on your people, your past performance, and the trust you've already built, and Municipal Alpha touches none of that.

So it is graded on the one thing it controls, surfacing real projects, sourced to the town's own record, with enough lead time to act. It tells you the work is coming, and where to look. What you do from there is yours.

Accuracy
"Isn't this in the permit databases and bid trackers already?"

The bid trackers and permit databases are summaries, they're compiled after the fact and a step removed from the actual record. They lag the workshop and the committee item by the weeks or months that are the whole point, and when a summary is wrong, it's usually wrong in the direction that costs you, a project mis-staged, a scope mis-stated, a date that has already moved.

Every Municipal Alpha signal is read from the town's own document and linked back to it, so the date, the dollar figure, and the stage are the record's, not a summary's. The standing offer on every signal is the same, open the source and check it yourself.

Show details for how the system reads it ▸

Every municipality in the country produces a stream of agenda packets, meeting minutes, capital plans, RFPs, bond warrants, siting hearings, and permit decisions. Most of them never get read by anyone outside the meeting room. The decisions that determine which water and wastewater work gets built, who is already advising on it, and when the money comes together almost all start in those documents, months before they reach a state DOT or revolving-fund work plan or the trade press.

Municipal Alpha reads those meetings the night the agenda drops, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies the documents, and follows the chain forward. A treatment-plant program shows up first as a line in a capital plan, then a consent-decree obligation, then an engineering contract, then a bid. A PFAS problem shows up first as a detection in a board update, then a pilot, then a treatment system, then a funded capital article. The system reads all of that, links it together, and surfaces the chain itself as the signal. For an engineering firm, that is the layer ahead of the RFP.
