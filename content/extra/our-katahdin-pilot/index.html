1. What we are watching

The scope is whatever you configure in the calibration sheet, which I have pre-filled from the action-words doc you sent and our conversation: your watch-towns, sector keywords, target companies, geography, policy comparables, and retention signals. You and Shane adjust it, set the priorities, and what comes back is the scope. The thing you configure is the thing you get, and it is what we measure against.

The goal of the sheet is to get started with enough to produce useful results, not to be exhaustively complete on day one. Put in the towns, words, and companies that matter most, enough for a useful first brief, and send it back. It does not need to be perfect, you already saw the shape of the results in the sample, so the first brief will look familiar, and the first week or two is the baseline we tune from, not the finished article.

From there, two things move at different speeds. Relevance, which of the surfaced items matter, sharpens continuously as you react to the weekly brief, that is the loop and where the real tuning happens, not in reworking this sheet. Scope, the set we watch, is the list you sent back, and we run with it rather than keep perfecting it, because the point is to get moving and let results show. Widening it in a real way, more towns, other states, eventually national, is the coverage step we take at the full term. So the pilot covers a good-enough starting set, and the full contract is where coverage grows.

Starting focus is your set across Maine. National analogs and complementary-industry matching are a later coverage add, kept out of the pilot to keep scope and cost sane.

Every item carries the same fields: Town · Source and date · What it is · Quote · Why it matters · Matched on · Source link · Status (new, updated, or unchanged) · Useful? (you mark it).

You get each week two ways: a readable email, and a CSV of the same items with those fields. The CSV is also the dial, you mark the Useful column on the ones that landed and send it back, and next week sharpens to them. It works like training a Spotify or Netflix feed, the more you tell it what hit, the more it serves up what matters and drops what does not, only here it is tuned on what Our Katahdin actually acts on.

2. What success looks like, and how we measure it

Three layers, separated by who controls what, so we both know what each of us is on the hook for. The weekly CSV is the measurement surface for all of it.

My end (measurable, and it is mine)

This is what you hold me to:

  • Coverage, every configured town and topic actually represented, no silent gaps.
  • Relevance, you flag what is useful and the feed tunes toward it. I am deliberately not chasing a "useful score" by sending only safe, obvious items, that would quietly cost you the small-jurisdiction catches that are the whole point. Relevance and completeness move together.
  • Earlier, for items that later surface publicly, how far ahead we flagged them, your surfaced date against when it hit the news. Date math on real events, so it cannot be inflated.
  • More complete, how many items came from the small jurisdictions you would not otherwise have seen. The counterweight that keeps the feed from quietly narrowing.
  • Actionable, every item carries a why-it-matters and a plausible next step. If I cannot name what you would do with it, it does not belong in the brief.
The shared proof (the one we read together)

You are acting on it, carrying items into tenant attraction, retention, and your regional and NBRC positioning, not just reading them. I measure it two ways: the Useful flags you send back each week, and a quick "which of these did you actually pursue" at the 30/60/90 check. It takes both of us, so I am not gated on it, but it is the truest signal the thing is working.

Outcomes (yours to close, I report the contribution)

A tenant lead, a retained employer, a stronger NBRC or EDA application where the brief played a part. I cannot close those for you, that is your work and your relationships, but I will show where something we surfaced was the early thread. One honest note: a look-back proves the method works, it does not prove a batting average. The live pilot proves the forward lead, by flagging things before the outcome and checking how early we were.

3. Terms

A simple paid pilot, no lock and no gate during the 90 days, it earns its place or it does not. $600 for the 90 days, handled up front to keep invoicing out of the way, which credits toward the ongoing term if we keep going. After the pilot, the ongoing nonprofit rate is $600 a month on a 12-month term for single-state coverage like yours, around 80% below the commercial rate. You can start whenever works on your end, funded however is easiest.

4. How we check it

A quick read at 30, 60, and 90 days, pointed back at this document: are the right towns and topics covered, is what we surface relevant and improving, are you acting on it, and what has it surfaced early. At 90 days it converts to the 12-month ongoing term or we shake hands and stop. No scorecard theater, just a shared read against what we agreed here.
