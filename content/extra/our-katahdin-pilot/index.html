1. What we are watching

The scope is whatever you configure in the calibration sheet, which I have pre-filled from the action-words doc you sent and our conversation: your watch-towns, sector keywords, target companies, geography, policy comparables, and retention signals. You and Shane adjust it, set the priorities, and what comes back is the scope. The thing you configure is the thing you get, and it is what we measure against.

You fill the sheet once, and that is the starting scope. It does not need to be perfect, it needs to be a good start, because from there the sharpening happens by reacting to the weekly brief, you tell me which items landed and which missed and it tunes, not by editing this sheet again. You already saw the shape of the results in the sample, so the first brief will look familiar. The first week or two is the baseline we tune from, not the finished article.

Two things move at different speeds here. Relevance, which of the surfaced items matter, sharpens continuously through that weekly loop. Scope, the set of towns, keywords, and companies we watch, is what you lock in the sheet, and we hold it fixed for the 90 days so the pilot stays a clean read. The list you send back is the definition, that is exactly what is covered. Widening it later, more towns, other states, eventually national, is the coverage step we take at the full term. So the pilot covers your core locked set, and the full contract is where coverage grows.

Starting focus is your locked set across Maine. National analogs and complementary-industry matching are a later coverage add, kept out of the pilot to keep scope and cost sane.

2. What success looks like

Three layers, deliberately separated by who controls what, so this is fair to hold both of us to.

What you hold me to (my job)

A weekly, source-linked brief covering your configured towns, filtered to your action-words and companies, every item linked to the municipal filing it came from. Over the 90 days it tunes to what you flag as useful, so relevance climbs and the noise drops. This is the part I own and can show from day one.

The proof it is working (ours together)

You are acting on it, carrying what we surface into tenant attraction, retention, and your regional and NBRC positioning, not just reading it. And you are earlier (ahead of things before they reach the news) and more complete (catching the small-jurisdiction items you would otherwise miss entirely).

Outcomes (yours to close, I report the contribution)

A tenant lead, a retained employer, a stronger NBRC or EDA application where the brief played a part. I cannot close those for you, that is your work and your relationships, but I will show where something we surfaced was the early thread. One honest note: a look-back proves the method works, it does not prove a batting average. The live pilot proves the forward lead, by flagging things before the outcome and checking how early we were.

3. Terms

A simple paid pilot, no lock and no gate during the 90 days, it earns its place or it does not. $600 for the 90 days, handled up front to keep invoicing out of the way, which credits toward the ongoing term if we keep going. After the pilot, the ongoing nonprofit rate is $600 a month on a 12-month term for single-state coverage like yours, around 80% below the commercial rate. You can start whenever works on your end, funded however is easiest.

4. How we check it

A quick read at 30, 60, and 90 days, pointed back at this document: are the right towns and topics covered, is what we surface relevant and improving, are you acting on it, and what has it surfaced early. At 90 days it converts to the 12-month ongoing term or we shake hands and stop. No scorecard theater, just a shared read against what we agreed here.
