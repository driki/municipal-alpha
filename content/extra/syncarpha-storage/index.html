The layer where a storage project lives or dies

A standalone storage project is won or lost at the local level long before it is public: a battery moratorium, a fire-code or setback ordinance written after a single thermal-runaway headline, a special-use denial at the planning board, a zoning-code amendment that quietly takes a class of parcel off the table. That layer is spread across hundreds of small jurisdictions, each moving on its own agenda, and almost no one aggregates it. By the time it reaches an RFP or a trade headline, the decision is already made.

I read that layer, every planning board and council and zoning board agenda, across the New York towns where this is being fought, every day.

Why not just do this in-house?

The fair question, and you should ask it. Here is the honest version: what an internal effort gets you, and where it runs out.

None of this is magic, and I will not pretend it is. For one town, one time, you can do it yourself. How hard that gets depends entirely on what you draw: one town hands you searchable agendas and clean text, the next buries the packet behind a vendor portal, in scanned images no parser likes, behind a crawler block, in a layout that changed since you last looked. You do not get to choose which one you get. Pick the town, fight through its site, get past the bot-blocks, parse the PDFs that do not want to be parsed, then strip the noise down to the handful of lines that actually decide a project. It is all doable. I have done it, town after town, the clean ones and the ugly ones, and built the machine that now does it on its own.

So the real question was never whether you can. It is whether it is worth your team rebuilding that machine and running it across your whole pipeline, versus pointing at the one that already does. Here is where a do-it-yourself version runs out:

  • It only watches the towns you already know to watch. The siting fight does not stay put. A moratorium one county over is the model the town you are bidding adopts a quarter later. I read every New York jurisdiction's planning, zoning, and council record, not the handful already on your list, so you see it coming before it is your problem.
  • The pull you did once is stale by the next meeting. A moratorium or a code amendment surfaces as a line in a committee agenda, once, weeks before the vote. A one-time read does not catch it. I scan the record every day, so the line gets caught when it posts, not the month after it passed, and the watch never skips the week someone is on a deal or out of office.
  • One town shows you one town. Reading all of it is what makes the pattern visible. Which towns are adopting which model law, where the fire-setback language is spreading, which way a county is turning. Your own towns can only ever tell you about your own towns.
  • Your analysts are worth more using this than collecting it. Wrangling town websites and re-pulling records is the low-value half of the job. Let me carry the collection, and your team spends its time on what it is actually good at: working the data, feeding it into your own siting models and tools, making the call. You augment what you have already built with the layer, you do not rebuild the plumbing to get it.
