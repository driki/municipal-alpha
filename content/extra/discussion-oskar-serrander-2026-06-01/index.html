Where the go-to-market actually stands

You read the decks, and you started doing the thing a good operator does on a cold deal, you asked why nobody has bought yet. This is my honest answer. Where the commercial side really is, my read on why nothing has closed, the moat objection you flagged, and the threads where your view would change what I do next.

Where the commercial side is, in five strokes

Skipping the product walkthrough, you have seen it. This is the go-to-market reality, point-in-time late May.

The wedge. Everyone with venture money in this space sells the same municipal substrate to government-vendor sales teams, helping vendors win public contracts. Pursuit raised $22M, Starbridge $42M, NationGraph $18M, roughly $80M combined. Municipal Alpha is the only one selling that substrate to investors instead, an early read on what municipal documents predict about tower, data-center, REIT, and credit activity. A data-sourcing lead at a multi-strategy fund named that exact cohort framing unprompted. A systematic-equity researcher at a large asset manager called the lead-to-lag relationship "very differentiated and not really available in the marketplace." The venture money validates the category. The wedge is the segment it is not chasing.

Traction, honestly. No revenue yet. The buyer-side thesis got its first outside proof this month, two champion-profile cold inbounds fifty minutes apart off a single data-marketplace listing, both still warm. About sixty active touchpoints across twelve segments, 211 prospect events in thirty days (eighty cold sends, forty-six replies, nine meetings booked, three pilots in early discussion). The funnel exists, but nothing has converted yet.

What is in play. REIT at $300K annual or a $30K ninety-day pilot, the live retail-REIT quote. Institutional alt-data at $50,000 to $200,000, the typical band for a marketplace-listed product. Tower at a $12K floor. Environmental and geotech at $5,000 to $15,000. Local news grant-funded or low-monthly. One conversion at REIT or institutional pricing lands the entire rest of the pipeline ceiling on its own.

The part that is differentiated as a business, not just a product. The corpus does double duty, signal source for the buyer and prospecting engine for the seller. Indexing tower filings surfaces which aggregators have portfolio exposure. The marketplace listing surfaces which funds are actively scouting for new data. Coverage is the lead-generation mechanism. Pattern 1: the shape of the data decides the reaction

The same corpus shown four different ways gets four different reactions, and the pattern is consistent across segments. The storyline chain wins everywhere. The raw feed and the narrow-deep cut lose.

Signal-chain storyline The upstream cause-and-effect chain (the Amherst NH 32-month tower chain, citizen comment to ground-lease vote) Warm, every time tower, systematic equity, geotech, media, PE, ED-org

The takeaway I read: the chain is the product, the firehose is not. The three cold demos are all the same failure, depth or volume without the narrative that makes it mean something. Systematic buyers want breadth plus a multi-year history floor, not one town read deeply.

Pattern 2: the objections cluster into five things

Eight logged objections across the conversations, and they repeat. A real sales motion should pre-empt each one before the buyer raises it.

  • "By the time it's in zoning, it's too late"
    A national tower operator. They do not want the zoning record, they want the trigger before the RFP. This both validates the upstream positioning and tells me which signals to drop. RFP pre-intelligence is where the group converged as highest value.
    National tower operator · timing objection
  • "I already have the FAA and FCC data"
    The tower operator's market-research lead pushed back on tower-filing data they already source. Do not sell a buyer the public dataset they already pull. Lead with the layer they cannot get.
    National tower operator · already-sourced objection
  • "It loses value if you sell my competitors"
    The tower operator's portfolio lead said he would pay more for exclusivity. That is a strategic-buyer tell, not just a pricing note, and it lines up with the acquisition-precursor read on this segment.
    National tower operator · exclusivity objection
  • "The raw feed is too noisy"
    A Northeast geotech engineering firm filters out construction below a dollar-value threshold and needs the same here. Filtering is the product. The firehose is a liability, which is the same lesson as Pattern 1.
    Northeast geotech firm · noise objection
  • "Who actually pays for this?"
    A public-policy analyst flagged that citizen-watchdog users will not pay. A Maine nonprofit's development director named the nonprofit budget reality, $3,000 to $25,000 a month is out of reach. Both are qualification signals, they tell me which segments to deprioritize, not product gaps.
    Public-policy and nonprofit · willingness-to-pay

The thing that is not clicking

My read: the top of the funnel works and the product lands on contact. The gap is the middle. There is no step that turns interest into a real, value-anchored opportunity before price enters the room. Four things stacked.

01I answered the price question instead of using it

"What's the cost" from a senior operator is a buying signal and an opening, not a spec to fill. The move is to turn it into discovery, "depends what you would point it at, what is the surprise that would have mattered most in the last year," build the value in their own portfolio, then anchor price to that. Answering with a number cold lets a buyer who has not yet seen the ROI quietly disqualify on price. This is your point. I think it is the biggest single lever.

02There is no human loop

The whole motion has been async email. No call, no relationship, no internal champion. These deals close because one person inside decides to fight for it, and I have been trying to transact where I should be recruiting.

03It is a genuinely new category, so there is no budget line

It sits upstream of what buyers already know. The state of the art is collecting RFPs and bids. This is the signal before that. That is a real edge, and it is also why an interested contact cannot buy, there is no procurement category to slot it into. The newness means the sale needs more discovery and a cheaper land-and-expand pilot, not a $300K cold ask. I am holding this as real friction, not as an excuse for not closing.

04Solo plus AI-build perception reads as vendor risk

A $5B company wondering "why sign a $300K annual with a one-person shop for something our data team could build" is a silent objection that kills the deal after the call ends. It has to be answered early, which is the next section.

The moat, and the "replicable AI build" objection

Your sharpest pushback was that it reads as an AI build that can be replicated, especially pitched as a solo, and that everyone has AI delusion right now. That objection lives in the VC pitch and the sales objection at the same time. Here is the answer, and you are right that it belongs on the first slide. Six stacked hard problems, in order.

  • Problem 1 · the universe
    A complete list of municipalities does not exist anywhere. I had to build it.
  • Problem 2 · the registry
    Which towns have websites, and which site belongs to which town. A registry that did not exist before this.
  • Problem 3 · the platform
    Identifying which software vendor runs each site, once you have found it.
  • Problem 4 · the crawlers
    A specialized crawler per vendor, because every vendor and every town configures its system differently.
  • Problem 5 · the extraction
    A custom extractor per document format, PDFs, Word files, photos of paper documents. Plus golden training sets and expert labeling to keep the models honest, because they confabulate without ground truth. Experts in the field did the classification work on the training data.
  • Problem 6 · the classification
    Telling an agenda from minutes from a master plan from a check register from a city-manager memo from genuine garbage like newsletters and library-circulation reports.

"It's public data" is true, and it is exactly the trap. Public is not the same as accessible. Validated on calls in almost these words, "these websites are a mess, you can never find anything." The defensibility is not secrecy, it is the assembled cost of six stacked hard problems at national scale, plus training data no weekend prototype reproduces.

The frame to flip: the $391 a month is proof the moat is real, one person built what is costing the venture-funded cohort roughly $80M and full teams, and they still build it for one buyer at a time on the vendor side. It is not evidence the thing is trivial. The open tension I want your read on, where is the line between selling the moat to a VC and handing them the playbook to go fund someone else to do it.

Hire the network, or crack the motion solo first

My own open question, the one I genuinely cannot call.

My honest read is that the gap looks more like a motion problem than a network problem. The cold email already reaches $5B-cap COOs. Hiring a connected vertical seller to fix a motion I have not proven means buying a process I cannot yet coach or evaluate. The sequence that compounds is to crack one repeatable close myself, get one logo and one paid pilot, then hire to scale a motion that is already known to work.

The real counter, and the reason I keep going back and forth, is that in relationship-driven markets like REITs and towers the network itself may be the unlock, and a year of solo grinding to learn what a connected hire knows on day one is its own cost. This is exactly the call I want your view on.
