The starting point from the prior thread

The April exchange with Matt narrowed where this product would actually be useful to your seat. Matt's answer was direct: the moment of truth is the utility load application, and a list of where those have been signed is the deliverable that would change a siting and origination strategy.

Matthew Kearns · 2026-04-15
"I'd like to know where a datacenter has signed a utility load application (application to be served power). If I had a list of those then I could figure out how to deliver power."

That answer crystallized the framing for this conversation. The utility load application is the decision window. The precursor chain that leads to it lives in the municipal record (EDA agendas, zoning overlays, planning-board site reviews, comp-plan amendments, town-meeting warrant articles, capital improvement plans) and surfaces six to twelve months earlier. Then it lands in the state PUC docket, the ISO queue, and the FAA OE/AAA filings. The system reads all of those layers and assembles the chain.

The signal stack, layer by layer

Each layer is a different upstream-or-downstream cut on the same project arc. The system reads them all and links across.

  • Layer 1 · Municipal
    Planning board agendas + minutes, EDA + economic-development committee discussions, comp-plan amendments and overlay-district drafts, town-meeting warrant articles, sealed-bid RFPs, capital improvement plans, abutter-notice mailings. The earliest signal that a town is being shopped, friendly toward, hostile to, or wobbly about a utility-scale project. Six to twenty-four months upstream of the load application.
  • Layer 2 · State legislative
    Active bills shaping where utility-scale solar, BESS, and data centers can go, indexed by state and by topic. Solar and storage moratoria, interconnection-process reforms, data-center load classification proposals, siting-and-permitting legislation. Shapes the rules-of-play envelope before a project is sited.
  • Layer 3 · State PUC
    Renewable-energy siting and interconnection dockets, utility rate cases that contain capital improvement plans, infrastructure investment plans and approvals. Indexed by docket, by municipality, by utility. Maine PUC, NH PUC, Massachusetts DPU, Connecticut PURA, Rhode Island PUC, Vermont PUC, NYSDPS. The layer immediately upstream of, and overlapping with, the utility load application.
  • Layer 4 · FAA OE/AAA
    Federal Obstruction Evaluation filings for structures above a height threshold. Solar disconnect and recloser poles, BESS substations, and the towers that accompany utility-scale solar interconnects all surface here. Downstream of permitting and upstream of construction. The right-now read on which competitor projects have advanced past the permitting layer and are about to build.
  • Layer 5 · Federal procurement
    SAM.gov solicitations indexed by agency and by topic. Substation O&M contracts, federal data-center construction at DOE / VA / DoD facilities, BLM solar site readiness, transmission-corridor work. Useful as a cross-reference for federal-customer load growth that pulls private utility-scale capacity behind it.

A worked chain, applied to your shape of project

The canonical demo chain in the corpus today is a thirty-two-month tower-lease arc in Amherst, NH, traceable from one citizen comment to a unanimous five-zero ground-lease vote. Same shape generalizes directly to a utility-scale solar or BESS siting arc: comp-plan amendment, EDA discussion, abutter outreach, PILOT term sheet, planning-board site plan, town-meeting warrant, final vote, then PUC docket and load application. The chain itself becomes the read.

  • Stage 1 · Citizen or developer-introduced item
    A line in a Board of Selectmen minutes file. Could be a resident raising the issue, or a developer's preliminary letter referenced in the agenda. Six to thirty months before the vote.
  • Stage 2 · Town-side institutional position
    Town counsel review, fire chief or DPW input, PILOT discussion, abutter notice. The substrate where towns reveal their posture toward the project type before any formal application.
  • Stage 3 · Site plan review and Planning Board hearing
    The first formal public read on the specific project at the specific site. Layer 1 surfaces both the agenda packet and the abutter responses.
  • Stage 4 · Town-meeting warrant article and vote
    In Maine, NH, and most of New England, the final authorization to lease or rezone goes through the warrant. The warrant article text, the explanatory note, and the final vote tally are all extracted.
  • Stage 5 · PUC docket and load application
    Layer 3 picks up where Layer 1 finishes. The PUC siting and interconnection docket lands, the utility load application is signed, the FAA filings for substations and interconnect poles follow. Layer 4 surfaces the construction-ready signal.

The chain reads forward across years, indexed by town, by project type, by competitor. Aaron's seat plugs in at Stage 5 today. The question for this conversation is how far back into Stages 1 through 4 it would be useful to see, and at what depth.

Where Municipal Alpha fits in the renewable-dev landscape

Three things tend to come up when a developer encounters this product. Worth naming them rather than letting them sit unsaid.

On Blumen and RenUSA and similar plays. Blumen is the environmental-and-regulatory layer at the moment of permitting. RenUSA is the community-engagement layer once a site has been picked. Municipal Alpha is the municipal-government layer in between, and it is the precursor read, the chain that precedes formal environmental review and community engagement by six to twenty-four months. Adjacent, not overlapping.

On in-house permitting teams. A DESRI-class developer already has people who can read these documents once they find them. The work the system does is not reading-the-document; it is surfacing-the-right-document at the right time across eighteen hundred municipalities simultaneously, with the precursor chain pre-assembled. The substrate is the moat, not the reading.

On the LLM commoditization concern. Fair concern. The defensible piece is not the LLM. It is the eighteen-hundred-municipality corpus, the navigator infrastructure that scrapes every CMS variant in production, the classification pipeline tuned across a year of buyer feedback, the precursor-chain logic that connects documents across years, and the FOAA back-channel for documents that are not posted online. A general-purpose LLM does not have the corpus and cannot get it cheaply.
