What Municipal Alpha is

Every city, town, county, and district in the country publishes the decisions that move infrastructure, capital, and credit before they happen: the franchise, the permit, the budget line, the vendor payment, the fund balance that moves a rating. Those records land months ahead of the public event, and they sit on roughly twenty thousand separate town websites, in incompatible formats, where ninety-nine percent of them never reach a search engine. So the people those decisions affect find out late, on the same day as everyone else, or they never find out at all.

Municipal Alpha reads all of them the night they post, across more than three thousand municipalities in multiple states, classifies what matters, verifies every signal against the source document, and routes it to whoever needs it early. The thing you would otherwise catch late, or never catch, arrives while there is still time to do something with it.

The obvious question
"In a year, given the pace of AI, can't an agent just do this? Set an alert on a model and get the same thing?"

Fair question, and the honest answer starts by conceding the part that is true: reading a document and classifying it is the cheap step, and it probably does commoditize. The value is in the four layers underneath it that a model starting from a prompt does not have.

  • The corpus. A model with perfect extraction skills starting today still does not have 2010 through 2026 on Monday. The history has to be accumulating now to exist in eighteen months. It has been.
  • The hard part is upstream of the model, and it is not AI work. Step zero is a registry mapping each local government to its actual digital footprint, the specific site or portal where its records live. Then you fingerprint that footprint to know which platform it runs (CivicPlus, Granicus, WordPress, Catalis, and a long tail) and how to pull from it. Then you extract, often from behind authenticated portals or PDFs indexed nowhere. Registry, fingerprinting, extraction: these are workflow and data-engineering problems, not LLM problems. The model only helps at the very end, once the document is already in hand.
  • A feed, not a question. You do not want to ask a model on Monday. You want something that fires the night the agenda posts. The product is a continuously running pipeline, not a query you have to remember to run.
  • The specific signal, and an honest one. Ask any model today which towns opened a competitive cable franchise this month, or approved a fiber easement. It cannot answer without the corpus and the resolved entities behind it, and if you ask anyway it will not tell you it does not know. It will confidently invent a town, a date, a citation. That is worse than no answer, because it looks like one. Every Municipal Alpha signal links back to the source document, so you get an answer you can defend instead of one you have to trust.

And if the bet is wrong and this does commoditize, the corpus accumulated by then is still the one a later entrant does not have. The cost of being early is overhead. The cost of being late is no corpus.

Show details for how the system reads it ▸

Every municipality in the country produces a stream of agenda packets, meeting minutes, RFPs, franchise renewals, pole-attachment agreements, easement authorizations, and broadband-committee minutes. Most of them never get read by anyone outside the meeting room. The decisions that determine where fiber gets built, which provider wins a town, and when the grant match comes together almost all start in those documents, months before they reach the FCC Broadband Data Collection, a public award list, or the trade press.

Municipal Alpha reads those meetings the night the agenda drops, across more than three thousand municipalities in multiple states, classifies the documents, and follows the chain forward. A competitive cable franchise shows up first as a single public-hearing notice, then a board review, then a signed ten-year agreement. A fiber route shows up first as an easement authorization on a council consent agenda. The system reads all of that, links it together, and surfaces the chain itself as the signal. For a broadband platform, that is the layer upstream of the map and the grant.

One town, thirty-two months, from a single citizen comment to a unanimous vote authorizing a thirty-year infrastructure ground lease. The example is a tower, because it is the cleanest fully-documented chain I have. Swap the tower for a fiber route, a competitive franchise, or a pole agreement and the shape is identical.
