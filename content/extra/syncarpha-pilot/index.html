Why a snapshot misleads, and why the solar signals tell you where storage is headed

A snapshot is a photo; these towns are a movie. And for storage there is a specific pattern worth knowing: towns build their energy-siting rules on solar first, then point the same machinery at batteries, often pushed along by battery fire-safety concern. So a town's solar activity is your early warning for the battery moratorium coming next.

Candor reads open for storage today, and that is exactly the part to watch. The town has regulated solar for years (a 2019 solar law, a community-solar project cleared review in 2022), so the rule-making machinery is already built. The only reason batteries are unscoped is that no one has turned that machinery on them yet. The trend around the town says that will not last.

In one of your five, a solar moratorium was followed within months by a parallel battery moratorium: the exact solar-then-storage sequence. A developer who checked between the two would have been told storage was fine, weeks before it was banned.

How earliness is measured. Lead time is arithmetic from dated documents, not a judgment: the gap between when I flag a change at its earliest public artifact (a hearing scheduled on an agenda, a referral, a draft local law) and the binding decision (the vote or the adoption). Both dates come from the documents, so the number is auditable, not asserted. I validate it two ways, both on my own ground. First, a retrospective backtest on changes that already happened: in one of your five towns, a battery moratorium's hearing was posted on the public agenda about four weeks before it was adopted, both dates straight from the record, provable today without you lifting a finger. Second, a forward catch during the pilot: at least one real change surfaced at its early stage with a documented lead time. Neither needs you to confirm it helped. The agenda is dated, the vote is dated, the lead time is the difference. It is a backtest of method and lead, not a forecast.
