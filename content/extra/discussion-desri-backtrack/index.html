The problem

"The load application is the part everyone can see. By then it is too late."

Energy-fund development desk, on where the signal has to be

A large load lands in a county long before the utility filing makes it official. First it is a quiet policy-discussion item on a town committee agenda, then a rezoning, then a fight over a moratorium, then a transmission or substation permit. It is all public, in the narrowest sense: a handful of people in a half-empty room, and only if you knew the item was on the agenda at all. After that the record sits unread on one of twenty thousand separate town websites. So the desk siting generation and storage around that load finds out at the load application, on the same day as everyone else, or never.

90,000+ local governments decide where large load lands, in public, the night they meet. No team could read them all. Municipal Alpha does, across more than three thousand of them, every night.

What Municipal Alpha is

Every city, town, county, and district in the country publishes the decisions that move infrastructure and load before they happen: the rezoning, the moratorium, the comprehensive-plan amendment, the transmission and substation permit, the economic-development agreement. Those records land months ahead of the public event, and they sit on roughly twenty thousand separate town websites, in incompatible formats, where ninety-nine percent of them never reach a search engine. So the people those decisions affect find out late, or never.

Municipal Alpha reads all of them the night they post, across more than three thousand municipalities in all fifty states, classifies what matters, verifies every signal against the source document, and routes it to whoever needs it early. The thing you would otherwise catch at the load application, or never catch, arrives while there is still time to position around it.

What it produces
Classified signals, each scored for confidence and linked back to the source record, assembled into the chain across time.
The method, not AI
A deterministic navigator and classification layer over a registry of where each government's records actually live. The model labels a document once it is in hand. It is not the moat.
The question a technical reader asks
"Given the pace of AI, can't an agent just do this? Point a model at the towns and get the same thing?"

Fair question, and the honest answer starts by conceding the part that is true: reading a document and classifying it is the cheap step, and it probably does commoditize. The value is in the layers underneath that a model starting from a prompt does not have.

  • The corpus. A model with perfect extraction skills starting today still does not have 2010 through 2026 on Monday. A backtrack needs the history to already exist. Ours has been accumulating.
  • The hard part is upstream of the model, and it is not AI work. Step zero is a registry mapping each local government to its actual digital footprint, the specific portal where its records live. Then you fingerprint that footprint to know the platform it runs (CivicPlus, Granicus, Catalis, a long tail) and how to pull from it. Then you extract, often from behind authenticated portals or PDFs indexed nowhere. Registry, fingerprinting, extraction: workflow and data-engineering problems, not LLM problems.
  • A feed, not a question. You do not want to ask a model on Monday. You want something that fires the night the agenda posts, across thousands of jurisdictions, continuously.
  • The specific signal, and an honest one. Ask any model today which counties opened a data-center moratorium this quarter. It cannot answer without the corpus and the resolved entities behind it, and it will not tell you it does not know. It will invent a town, a date, a citation. Every Municipal Alpha signal links back to the source document, so you get an answer you can defend, not one you have to trust.

And if the bet is wrong and this does commoditize, the corpus accumulated by then is still the one a later entrant does not have. The cost of being early is overhead. The cost of being late is no corpus.

What a moratorium chain means for a siting desk

An honest read. This particular chain is a project being fought, not built. For DESRI that is still signal, in two directions.

  • Demand pressure, located early. Someone proposed a large load in Bangor. Whether it lands or routes elsewhere, you knew in March that this jurisdiction is in play, before the load application names it.
  • A moratorium is itself a siting input. It tells you this jurisdiction is closing, so the load reroutes to a neighbor. That is the "to and from" of power across a region, exactly the cross-boundary movement worth tracking.
  • The signal fires regardless of outcome. The product is not "a data center will be built here." It is "the municipal record is moving here, months before the part you can see."

Questions where your read changes what I build

Genuine discussion topics, not a checklist. Witter's answers define the product for this use case.

Data in, data out: you hold a thesis that a large load is coming to a county. What exactly do you want back, the surrounding-municipality precursor permits and upgrades, the moratorium and rezoning activity, the transmission and substation filings, or all of it as one chain?
This is the load-bearing question. It decides whether the deliverable is a backtrack report, a structured precursor dataset, or an ongoing watch, and it is the difference between a useful tool and a data dump.
What do you read today to form one of these theses, and where did the last one come from that surprised you?
You found a power-line buildout once by reading a lithium-mine proposal. The surfaces you already mine by hand are coverage targets, and the last surprise defines what "early enough" means.
Deliverable format: a structured data dump, a written backtrack, or a feed into your GIS and systems?
Nicc asked this directly. The delivery surface matters more than the signal for adoption. A brilliant precursor in the wrong format is invisible inside the systems you run.
Coverage, depth, and recency: which states and regions, how far back the chain needs to go, and whether this is a one-time backtrack or ongoing precursor monitoring, at what cadence?
The three vectors price the work. A national one-time backtrack and a monthly multi-region watch are different products. Naming them keeps the pilot scoped.
Cross-state tracking: a CT RFP can land projects in other states, with load filed after bids are awarded. Is tracking that "to and from" of power across state lines part of what you need?
Nicc named this as a pain. If it is in scope, the product is a multi-state chain, not a single-county pull, and the corpus is already national.
