<p class="cs-intro">
Renewable energy developers face a fragmented regulatory landscape. Solar ordinances are written at the municipal level, evolve through public meetings, and aren't published in any central registry until they're already adopted. <strong>By then, the competitive window is closed.</strong> Municipal Alpha tracks the regulatory pipeline across 2,500+ municipalities. This case study traces the moratorium-to-permit pattern through four towns where solar development was debated, restricted, and eventually regulated. Every citation links to a verifiable public document.
</p>

<div class="cs-window">
<h3>The pattern generalizes</h3>
<p>Solar is the most common moratorium trigger right now, but the moratorium-to-permit pipeline applies to any infrastructure-intensive development that municipalities haven't regulated yet:</p>
<p><span class="cs-highlight">Data centers</span> as hyperscaler demand hits rural power grids. <span class="cs-highlight">Battery storage</span> follows solar by 12-18 months. <span class="cs-highlight">EV charging</span> where municipal zoning hasn't caught up. <span class="cs-highlight">Cannabis facilities</span>, the original New England moratorium archetype.</p>
<p>Each follows the same institutional path: applications arrive that don't fit existing zoning, the town reacts with a moratorium, a study committee writes new rules, and the rules create a framework that either enables or kills the next wave. The documents are public at every stage.</p>
</div>
