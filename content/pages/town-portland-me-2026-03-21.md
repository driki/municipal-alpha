## City Government

Portland received a significant federal grant this week, with the Department of Health and Human Services awarding the city $76,000. The funds are earmarked for public health initiatives, expected to bolster existing programs aimed at community wellness and support for vulnerable populations across the city. (Federal Grant 2026-HHS-001)

Infrastructure development was also a focus, as new submissions were made to the Maine Public Utilities Commission Docket 2025-00151. This docket concerns Central Maine Power's ongoing infrastructure investment plans, which will impact the reliability and modernization of the power grid for Portland residents and businesses. Additionally, the Federal Aviation Administration issued two new tower filings, 2026-ANE-191-NRA and 2026-ANE-192-NRA, for two proposed 50-foot towers (60 feet Above Ground Level) within city limits. These notices are standard procedure for evaluating potential airspace obstructions, likely for telecommunications or utility infrastructure.

The City Council convened its regular meeting on Monday, March 17, with discussions centered on affordable housing initiatives. City Manager John P. Smith provided an update on the city's strategies to address the persistent development pressure and expand housing options. Councilors also reviewed public input regarding potential updates to the city's energy efficiency codes, a regulatory area gaining increased attention statewide. (March 17 City Council Minutes). The Planning Board remains active, with multiple development proposals currently under review, contributing to its pipeline of eight active documents.

## Schools

The Portland Public Schools Board of Education met on Tuesday, March 18, to continue its review of the Fiscal Year 2027 budget proposal. Superintendent Eleanor Vance presented updated enrollment projections, highlighting a slight increase in kindergarten registration for the upcoming academic year. Discussions included allocations for special education services and proposed capital improvements to Deering High School's athletic facilities. The board anticipates a final budget vote in April after further public hearings.

## Police

Portland Police Department officers responded to 187 calls for service during the week of March 15-21. On Sunday, March 16, officers investigated a report of shoplifting at a retail establishment in the Old Port district, leading to the apprehension of a suspect. On Wednesday, March 19, a minor two-vehicle collision occurred at the intersection of Forest Avenue and Deering Avenue, resulting in no serious injuries but causing temporary traffic delays. Additionally, officers addressed multiple noise complaints near the University of Southern Maine campus on Friday evening.

## Fire & EMS

The Portland Fire Department and Emergency Medical Services responded to 93 calls this week, including 68 for medical aid and 25 for fire-related incidents. On Monday, March 17, firefighters extinguished a small kitchen fire at a residence on Washington Avenue in the East Deering neighborhood, with damage contained to the kitchen area. No injuries were reported. Later in the week, on Thursday, March 20, a medical emergency on Peaks Island required interagency coordination, with EMS personnel transporting a patient via ferry to Maine Medical Center for advanced care. The department also provided mutual aid to South Portland for a commercial building alarm on Friday.

## Real Estate

Transactions recorded with the Cumberland County Registry of Deeds this week reflect a continued active market. Among the notable sales: 140 Eastern Promenade, a three-bedroom waterfront condominium, sold for $1,250,000. On Peaks Island, a four-bedroom island home located at 25 Bay View Avenue changed hands for $850,000. A two-unit investment property at 87 Congress Street in the Munjoy Hill neighborhood was purchased for $450,000, and a three-bedroom single-family residence at 33 West Street sold for $620,000.

## Permits

The city's Inspections Division issued several permits during the week, indicating ongoing development and renovation activity. These included: a commercial interior renovation permit for 240 Commercial Street, a new storefront sign permit for 12 Elm Street, and a residential solar panel installation permit for 45 Oak Street. Other permits issued included a deck addition at 18 Willow Street, a permit for the demolition and rebuild of a garage at 7 Harbor View Road, and a restaurant kitchen upgrade permit for 120 Brighton Avenue.
